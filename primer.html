A Primer

The Balanced Housing Framework.

A new architecture for California housing.

Common Ground

What we share.

Every Californian can see what the current housing system is producing. The teacher commuting two counties to work. The nurse priced out of the town where she runs the ER. The neighborhoods changing in ways no one chose. The lawsuits where homes should be.

The Legislature has worked hard on this for a decade. So have the cities, the builders, the housing advocates, the environmentalists. Good people, in good faith, working inside a system that produces this anyway.

This is not a failure of effort. It's a failure of architecture.

What Needs to Change

Architecture, not effort.

The current rules pit the State against cities, mandates against capacity, builders against communities. When the system itself is built for conflict, no amount of effort or good faith can produce aligned outcomes. Change the design, and the same people produce different results.

The Balanced Housing Framework is that architecture.

A New Direction

The inversion.

The current system tries to bring people to opportunity — forcing density into the most expensive places, overriding local planning to make it happen.

The Framework brings opportunity to people.

The State stops being an enforcer and becomes a partner. It invests in communities that want to grow, funds the infrastructure that makes growth livable, and helps create the next generation of California where it can actually be built — with the workforce living in the places where they work, in homes they can afford.

A simple structural sequence:

Capacity first+investment=workforce housing

Every other provision exists to make that sequence enforceable.

Current System
  • State mandates housing growth top-down
  • ↓
  • Cities forced to absorb without capacity or funding
  • ↓
  • Luxury units, litigation, no workforce homes
The Framework
  • Communities measure their own capacity
  • ↓
  • State invests in the growth communities choose
  • ↓
  • Workforce housing built where infrastructure exists

The inversion: from top-down mandate to partnership-led investment.

Constitutional Architecture

The constitutional anchor.

California is one of eighteen states where citizens can write rules directly into the state constitution — rules that outlast any future legislature or administration, regardless of party. The Coastal Commission was installed this way in 1972. Proposition 13 in 1978. Both are still standing.

One sentence anchors the new architecture:

“
The State of California shall not impose housing mandates on any community without first establishing its physical capacity and funding the infrastructure required to support its growth.
Proposed Constitutional Amendment

Once enacted, it stands the way the Coastal Commission stands — beyond the reach of any one Sacramento cycle.

The Mechanism

The four moves.

I
Move One

Communities measure their own capacity.

Real engineering — streets, water, sewer, evacuation routes, schools. Peer-reviewed standards. Numbers a court can verify.

Measurement before mandate.

II
Move Two

The State funds the growth communities choose.

Investment flows to communities that want to build — bringing roads, schools, transit, and emergency services to places that have been ready to grow but starved of resources.

Partnership where there used to be pressure.

III
Move Three

Cities decide whether to grow — and on what terms.

No community is forced to add housing. A city that grows accepts one constitutional condition: a share of new homes stays accessible to the workforce the community depends on — teachers, nurses, tradespeople, first responders. Not just at the moment of construction — at every sale, for as long as the home stands.

Affordable at every sale. For as long as the home stands.

IV
Move Four

Builders get a clock.

Complete applications that meet a city's standards get approved on a published timeline — with automatic approval if the city misses the window. Predictability replaces litigation.

Permits become homes.

Aligned Outcomes

What alignment produces.

When the architecture aligns, each constituency gets what they actually wanted.

Working Families
Homes they can afford in the communities where they work.
Existing Residents
The social contract of homeownership honored, decisions made locally.
Growth-Welcoming Communities
Investment to become real communities instead of commuter overflow.
Cities That Choose Growth
Infrastructure, control over what growth looks like, a defined endpoint to the housing obligation.
Builders
Projects that finance against a clock instead of a court calendar.
The State
Housing production instead of housing litigation.
Environmentalists
Growth routed to where infrastructure exists, designed around the systems it depends on.

These aren't compromises. They aren't trades where everyone gets a little less. They're what happens when the rules align the interests that the current system forces into conflict — and balance follows.

Where You Come In

An invitation.

The Framework is being prepared for the 2030 ballot. Legal counsel is stress-testing the architecture. Academic reviewers are vetting the constitutional design. The coalition is forming across the constituencies the Framework serves — cities, working families, environmental partners, infrastructure unions, affordable housing advocates.

If you've been working on California housing — in the Legislature, at HCD, in city hall, in the building trades, in the advocacy community — this is an architecture you can walk toward.

If you've been watching this work for a decade and suspecting the answer is structural — this is that.
